The problem

When seconds matter, fragmentation becomes dangerous.

Merry Health's emergency coordination team was running three separate tools to manage a single dispatch: a legacy CAD system for incident logging, a spreadsheet for unit availability, and a phone line for hospital capacity. None of them talked to each other.

During a mass casualty event — exactly when the system needed to work — coordinators were switching tabs, checking rows, and making phone calls simultaneously while managing 8–12 active incidents. Decisions were delayed. Vehicles were misrouted. Staff were burning out.

The error wasn't human. It was architectural. The system was designed around data entry, not decision-making. It gave coordinators information — it just gave it to them in three different places, in three different formats, at three different moments.

The brief: build one system that puts everything a coordinator needs to dispatch correctly in front of them, exactly when they need it, in a format they can process under pressure.

The context

The system already existed. It just wasn't designed.

Merry Health wasn't starting from zero. They had a CAD system, unit tracking, and hospital capacity data. The problem wasn't missing data — it was that no one had ever designed the layer between the data and the coordinators who needed to act on it.

Each tool was built by a different team, for a different purpose. The CAD was built by engineers for logging. The spreadsheet was built by coordinators as a workaround. The phone line existed because neither of the other two could answer "which hospital has a bed right now?"

The challenge wasn't technical — it was design. Someone needed to figure out what coordinators actually needed to see, when they needed to see it, and what action they needed to take. That had never been done. There was zero UX research on coordinator mental models.

Discovery

Understanding pressure before designing screens.

I spent 4 shifts embedded with coordinators before drawing a single wireframe. Not to watch them use the software — to watch them make decisions.

The most useful thing I learned in the first shift: coordinators don't read during surge. They scan. They use colour, position, and size to triage information — not labels, not numbers, not text. A system that requires reading at peak demand will be ignored.

The second most useful thing: coordinators have a mental hierarchy of what matters. First: is anyone going to die in the next 4 minutes? Second: is there a unit that can reach them? Third: is there a hospital that can take them? The system needed to answer those three questions in that order, automatically.

The research also surfaced something unexpected: coordinators didn't want a system that made decisions for them. They wanted a system that made their decisions faster. The distinction matters enormously for design. The interface needed to recommend, not automate — and it needed to show its reasoning, or experienced coordinators would override it every time.

"I don't need the system to be smarter than me. I need it to be faster than three phone calls."
"If I can't see why it's suggesting Unit 14, I'll ignore it and pick myself. Every. Single. Time."

Four insights reshaped the product direction.

After 4 shifts and 12 hours of debrief interviews, four findings changed the design direction entirely.

Insight 01
Coordinators process by position, not by label

During surge, coordinators navigate entirely by spatial memory. "The urgent ones are always top-left." Any design that reorders content dynamically breaks their mental model. Fixed positions, not sorted lists.

Insight 02
Recommendation without reasoning is worthless

Every suggestion the system makes needs a visible reason — ETA, capacity, skill match. Without it, coordinators override by default. Trust is built incrementally, and only through transparency.

Insight 03
The handoff moment is the highest-risk moment

When a coordinator hands off a shift to a colleague, incidents get dropped. The system needed to make shift handoff a first-class feature — a structured summary, not a verbal briefing over paper notes.

Insight 04
Hospital capacity is the invisible bottleneck

The right unit dispatched to the wrong hospital costs more time than a slower unit going to the right one. Hospital capacity had to be a primary input in dispatch recommendations, not an afterthought confirmed by phone.

Adaptive before automation.

The design principle that shaped every decision: the system adapts to the coordinator, not the other way around. Automation comes later — only after the adaptive layer works.

The design

One operational source of truth.

The redesigned system collapses three tools into a single, always-current view. No tabs. No cross-referencing. No phone calls to confirm what the screen should already know.

Designed for moments of loss.

Three features designed specifically for the hardest moments — mass casualty events, shift handoffs, and hospital capacity failures.

Feature 01

Surge mode

One-click mode that strips the interface to essential controls only. Hides non-critical notifications, collapses secondary panels, and increases incident card size by 40% — optimised for the 8-12 incident load of an MCI event.

"I can't read anything during a surge. I need bigger, simpler, fewer."
Feature 02

Structured shift handoff

At handoff time, the system generates a live summary: active incidents, units in transit, pending decisions, and unconfirmed hospital beds. Incoming coordinator reviews and accepts before the previous one signs off. No dropped incidents.

"Handoffs are where things get lost. There's no standard — it's whoever has time to brief."
Feature 03

Hospital capacity layer

Live integration with hospital ED capacity, surfaced directly on the dispatch recommendation. If the nearest hospital is at capacity for the incident type, the system recommends the second-nearest with capacity. No phone calls.

"The worst outcome isn't a slow dispatch. It's arriving at a hospital that can't take the patient."
Feature 04

One-tap dispatch with undo

Confirming a dispatch is a single tap with a 5-second undo window. Removed every confirmation dialog from the critical path. For a decision made 40+ times per shift, friction compounds into meaningful delay.

"Every extra click when I'm in the middle of a surge is a second I can't get back."

Constraints shaped the product.

Three constraints that most designers would try to work around became the product's core strengths instead.

Constraint 01

Aging hardware

The dispatch centre runs on machines from 2016 with low-refresh monitors. No animation-heavy UIs. No live map rerendering. No Canvas. The constraint forced the design into a layout that's genuinely faster to parse — because it has no visual noise.

"If it looks fancy, it probably won't run. We've been burned by that before."
Constraint 02

No time for training

Coordinators can't take training days. The system had to be learnable in one shift. This forced every interaction to be self-evident — no tooltips, no onboarding flows, no documentation required. Labelling and layout had to carry all the weight.

"We don't have time to train people. They need to figure it out on the job."
Constraint 03

Experts, not beginners

The users are domain experts. They don't need the system to explain emergency medicine to them. They need it to remove the friction between their expertise and the outcome. Every "helpful" UI affordance that slowed an expert down was cut.

"I've done this for 11 years. I don't need the software to explain what a cardiac arrest is."
Constraint 04

Six weeks, not six months

A 6-week timeline forced ruthless prioritisation. I built a single scoring rubric: impact on decision speed × frequency of use × technical feasibility. Anything that didn't score in the top 40% was explicitly deferred with a documented reason.

Scope discipline is a design skill. Not everything deferred was a failure — most of it was correct prioritisation.

What this project taught me.

Three things I now apply on every high-stakes operational design problem.

Lesson 01
Research in the environment, not about it

Reading about emergency coordination and observing it are two entirely different inputs. The four shifts I spent embedded changed almost every assumption I brought in. No amount of desk research would have surfaced "coordinators don't read during surge."

Lesson 02
Transparent systems earn trust; opaque ones get ignored

Every time the design surfaced a recommendation without showing its reasoning, coordinators said they'd override it. Trust in a system is earned through legibility — make the reasoning visible, and experienced users will start relying on it.

Lesson 03
Constraints are design material, not obstacles

The aging hardware constraint that I initially tried to work around became the reason the final interface was faster to parse than anything I'd have designed without it. The no-training constraint produced more intuitive labelling than any usability test would have.
